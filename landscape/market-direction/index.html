Understanding CPS: OT, ICS, and SCADA Explained

Cyber-physical systems (CPS) is the term redefining how vendors frame OT, ICS, and SCADA security. Understanding what it covers — and what it can obscure — is essential for evaluating whether a platform actually protects your environment.

CPS: the umbrella term

Cyber-physical systems is the broadest possible framing: any system where software and physical processes are tightly integrated. It encompasses OT, ICS, SCADA, IoT, medical devices, and building systems under a single term. Claroty's 2022 rebrand from OT security to CPS protection was the clearest signal that the market was converging around this language, and other major vendors have followed.

The shift reflects a genuine market evolution. As IT/OT convergence has accelerated and IoT devices have proliferated across industrial environments, the boundaries between traditional industrial OT and other connected physical systems have blurred. A factory floor now routinely includes legacy PLCs running proprietary protocols alongside modern IoT sensors streaming data to cloud analytics platforms. A platform claiming CPS coverage is signaling that it protects across that entire spectrum.

For evaluation purposes, a CPS platform claim is broad by definition. Verify the depth of coverage in your specific environment — particularly ICS protocol support and deployment constraints for legacy industrial assets — rather than taking the CPS label at face value. Breadth and depth are different capabilities, and the vendors with the widest CPS coverage are not always the ones with the deepest OT expertise.

How the terms relate

OT, ICS, and SCADA are nested subsets, all sitting under the CPS umbrella alongside IoT, medical devices, and building systems.

CPS All systems where software and physical processes are tightly integrated
├ OT All technology that monitors or controls physical processes
├ ICS Industrial automation and control systems specifically
├ SCADA Distributed supervisory control — pipelines, grids, water networks
├ DCS Distributed control systems — refineries, chemical plants, process industries
└ PLC / RTU Programmable logic controllers and remote terminal units — discrete manufacturing, field devices
├ IoT Connected sensors, actuators, and edge devices — industrial and commercial
├ Medical Connected medical devices and healthcare infrastructure
└ BMS Building management systems — HVAC, access control, physical security

What vendor terminology signals during evaluation

Vendors use these terms to define the scope of what their products protect. The signals are not always accurate, and the same platform may use different terminology in different sales contexts. Here is what each term typically implies.

CPS platform
Broadest possible coverage claim. The vendor is signaling that their platform sees across OT, IoT, medical, and building systems in a unified view. Verify protocol depth in your specific industrial environment — CPS breadth often comes at the cost of ICS-specific depth. Ask specifically about coverage for the protocols your equipment uses.
OT security platform
Broad industrial coverage claim without the IoT and medical device framing. Typically signals stronger roots in traditional industrial environments. Verify whether coverage extends to your specific OT stack — the protocols, legacy assets, and deployment constraints that define your environment.
ICS security specialist
Depth signal. The vendor is claiming expertise specifically in industrial control system environments. This typically means stronger protocol coverage for ICS protocols (Modbus, Profinet, EtherNet/IP, DNP3) and more experience with the operational constraints of factory and plant environments — passive-only monitoring, no active scanning, air-gapped deployment options.
SCADA security
Vertical signal. Strong coverage for distributed infrastructure environments — energy, utilities, pipelines, water. May have weaker coverage for discrete manufacturing or process industry environments that use DCS architectures rather than SCADA. Check protocol support for IEC 61850, IEC 60870, and DNP3 if you operate grid or utility infrastructure.
